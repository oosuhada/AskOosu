## 1. Core Positioning

### 1-1. One-Line Positioning

Oosu is an **AI-native product builder** who uses AI agents not just for code generation, but as working partners across product framing, UX structure, implementation, debugging, documentation, and answer-quality improvement.

### 1-2. Short Positioning

Oosu's edge is not “replacing an entire team with AI.” It is the ability to use AI agents to connect product thinking, UX flow, implementation, documentation, and validation faster — while still keeping human judgment responsible for direction and quality.

In the AI era, teams may not disappear, but the minimum viable team size for early product validation will likely shrink. Oosu is preparing for that environment: in small projects, he can work like a product owner with AI agents; in larger teams, he can connect PM, design, engineering, and AI execution.

### 1-3. Long Positioning

Oosu believes the way products are built will change significantly in the AI era. In the past, planning, design, frontend, backend, documentation, and testing had to be split across multiple people because the scope was too large for one person to handle well.

As AI-agent workflows mature, one person can explore a much wider surface area: turning an idea into a product brief, UI flow, database/API structure, code draft, debugging plan, documentation, and evaluation checklist. This does not mean people become unnecessary. It means the human role moves upward: from pure execution volume toward problem definition, judgment, validation, and responsibility.

Oosu's edge sits exactly there. He does not use AI only as a code-writing shortcut. He uses AI as a partner across product planning, UX structuring, frontend/backend implementation, RAG source design, answer guardrails, debugging, documentation, and post-build improvement.

That makes him less of a narrow specialist and more of a product-minded AI-connected fullstack builder. In team settings, he can help connect PM, design, and engineering language. In smaller product units, he can operate closer to a product owner — directing AI agents, making decisions, and validating output quickly.

## 2. Key Thesis Statements

### Thesis 1. Teams will not disappear, but the minimum viable team size will shrink

Oosu does not need to claim that team projects will vanish. The safer and stronger thesis is that **early product validation will require fewer people than before**. AI agents allow one person to explore planning, design, development, testing, and documentation faster than traditional workflows.

### Thesis 2. The boundaries between roles will matter less in early product work

Frontend, backend, design, PM, and QA will remain real disciplines. But in early-stage product work, the ability to understand and connect those disciplines becomes more valuable. Someone who can move across product, UX, data, and implementation can validate ideas faster.

### Thesis 3. Human value shifts from execution volume to judgment

As AI increases execution speed, the human differentiator becomes judgment: what to build, what not to build, how to evaluate quality, how to reduce risk, how to preserve user trust, and when to ask a human expert for deeper review.

### Thesis 4. AI-native builders should collaborate better, not avoid people

Using AI well should not make someone anti-team. It should make collaboration more concrete. AI-generated prototypes, flows, documents, and implementation drafts can help teams discuss real artifacts instead of vague ideas.

### Thesis 5. Oosu's edge is AI-native product ownership

Oosu's strength is not deep specialization in one technical layer. His current value is integration: user problems, business context, UX flow, frontend implementation, backend/API logic, data/RAG structure, and AI answer quality as one connected product experience.

Oosu uses AI agents for product framing, requirement breakdown, code drafts, debugging, refactoring, documentation, and test thinking. The human role is to define the goal, provide constraints, review the result, and decide whether the output is good enough to ship.

**Default Answer**

Working with AI agents does not simply mean asking AI to write code.

Oosu starts by defining the problem, user flow, constraints, and desired output. Then he uses AI agents to draft implementation plans, code, UI copy, documentation, debugging hypotheses, or test cases.

1. Define the problem and user context.
2. Break the work into product, UI, data, API, and edge cases.
3. Brief the AI agent with context, constraints, and file structure.
4. Generate drafts: code, flow, copy, documentation, or test ideas.
5. Review the result: code flow, logs, types, runtime behavior, UX, and requirements.
6. Iterate through errors and missing details.
7. Document decisions and remaining risks.

In AskOosu, this applies not only to code, but also to RAG source design, FAQ answer banks, fallback behavior, answer guardrails, and recruiter-safe response quality.
